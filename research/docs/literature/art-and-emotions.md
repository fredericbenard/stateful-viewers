# Art and emotions: what the research says

Research on **art and emotions** is now a large interdisciplinary field spanning psychology, neuroscience, philosophy, and aesthetics. The consensus is not a single theory but a **set of converging findings** about how art evokes, shapes, and regulates emotional experience.

Below is a structured overview of what the research broadly says, and how it connects to the **Stateful Viewers** project.

---

## 1) Art is fundamentally an emotional experience

Across centuries of theory and modern empirical work, one core claim keeps reappearing:

- Emotional experience is **central, not incidental**, to art perception.
- In empirical aesthetics, emotional responses to art are treated as a **special case of general human emotion and motivation**.

This means art isn't just about cognition (recognizing objects, interpreting meaning), but about **felt experience**—often complex, layered, and dynamic.

### Key sources

- Silvia, P. J. (2005). Emotional responses to art: From collation and arousal to cognition and emotion. *Review of General Psychology*, 9(4), 342–357.
- Leder, H., Belke, B., Oeberst, A., & Augustin, D. (2004). A model of aesthetic appreciation and aesthetic judgments. *British Journal of Psychology*, 95(4), 489–508.
- Pelowski, M., Markey, P. S., Forster, M., Gerger, G., & Leder, H. (2017). Move me, astonish me… delight my eyes and brain: The Vienna Integrated Model of top-down and bottom-up processes in Art Perception (VIMAP) and corresponding affective, evaluative, and neurophysiological correlates. *Physics of Life Reviews*, 21, 80–125.

## 2) Art evokes a wide and distinctive range of emotions

Research shows that the emotional palette of art is **broader than everyday emotion categories**:

- **Aesthetic emotions** include awe, being moved, wonder, interest, confusion, pleasure, sadness, nostalgia, chills, and others that don't map neatly onto basic emotion taxonomies.
- These emotions are not just "positive"—people can enjoy sadness, tension, or even fear in art contexts. This is sometimes called the **paradox of negative emotions in art**.

Importantly:

- Emotions in art are often **mixed, ambiguous, or evolving over time**, not single discrete states.
- **Bodily responses** (chills, tears, arousal patterns) are part of the experience and have been studied as markers of aesthetic engagement.

This aligns strongly with the phenomenological idea that aesthetic experience unfolds over time—something very close to Stateful Viewers' framing.

### Key sources

- Menninghaus, W., Wagner, V., Hanich, J., Wassiliwizky, E., Kuehnast, M., & Jacobsen, T. (2015). Towards a psychological construct of being moved. *PLoS ONE*, 10(6), e0128451.
- Keltner, D., & Haidt, J. (2003). Approaching awe, a moral, spiritual, and aesthetic emotion. *Cognition and Emotion*, 17(2), 297–314.
- Menninghaus, W., Wagner, V., Wassiliwizky, E., Jacobsen, T., & Koelsch, S. (2017). The Distancing–Embracing model of the enjoyment of negative emotions in art reception. *Behavioral and Brain Sciences*, 40, e347.
- Eerola, T., & Vuoskoski, J. K. (2011). A comparison of the discrete and dimensional models of emotion in music. *Psychology of Music*, 39(1), 18–49.

## 3) Emotional response to art is shaped by context, knowledge, and personal history

Emotion is not a fixed reaction to a stimulus; it is **situated**:

- **Prior knowledge** (art training, familiarity with a style, cultural background) changes what emotions arise and how intensely.
- **Physical context** (museum vs screen, alone vs with others, time pressure vs leisure) modulates affective engagement.
- **Personal relevance** (autobiographical resonance, identity, value sensitivity) can amplify or transform emotional response.
- **Expectation and framing** (knowing the title, reading a wall text, hearing a guide) can redirect emotional attention before any direct encounter with the work.

This is the domain of **reception theory** (Jauss, Iser): the viewer is not a blank slate but brings a "horizon of expectations" that conditions what is felt.

### Key sources

- Jauss, H. R. (1982). *Toward an aesthetic of reception* (T. Bahti, Trans.). University of Minnesota Press.
- Iser, W. (1978). *The act of reading: A theory of aesthetic response*. Johns Hopkins University Press.
- Leder, H., & Nadal, M. (2014). Ten years of a model of aesthetic appreciation and aesthetic judgments: The aesthetic episode—developments and challenges in empirical aesthetics. *British Journal of Psychology*, 105(4), 443–464.
- Bullot, N. J., & Reber, R. (2013). The artful mind meets art history: Toward a psycho-historical framework for the science of art appreciation. *Behavioral and Brain Sciences*, 36(2), 123–137.

## 4) Temporal dynamics: emotion unfolds, it doesn't just "happen"

Aesthetic experience is not instantaneous. Research on **temporal dynamics** shows:

- **First impressions** (within ~500ms–2s) involve rapid affective appraisal (fluency, novelty detection, automatic valence).
- **Elaborative processing** (seconds to minutes) brings in meaning-making, memory, narrative, and deeper emotional differentiation.
- **Cumulative effects** across a sequence of works (e.g., walking through a gallery) are real: contrast, fatigue, priming, and mood carry-over all shape response to each subsequent work.

The experience of a single artwork can itself unfold in phases: initial attraction or puzzlement → deeper engagement or resistance → resolution or lingering ambiguity.

### Relevance to Stateful Viewers

This is the core theoretical motivation for the project's architecture. Rather than treating each image as an independent stimulus, the system carries forward **internal state** so that prior encounters condition subsequent ones. The state schema (mood, tension, energy, openness) is a simplified proxy for these temporal dynamics.

### Key sources

- Dewey, J. (2005). *Art as experience*. Perigee Books. (Original work published 1934)
- Locher, P., Krupinski, E. A., Mello-Thoms, C., & Nodine, C. F. (2007). Visual interest in pictorial art during an aesthetic experience. *Spatial Vision*, 21(1–2), 55–77.
- Vessel, E. A., Starr, G. G., & Rubin, N. (2012). The brain on art: Intense aesthetic experience activates the default mode network. *Frontiers in Human Neuroscience*, 6, 66.

## 5) The role of ambiguity: unresolved states are aesthetically productive

A consistent finding is that **ambiguity is not noise**—it is an aesthetic resource:

- Works that are neither trivially understandable nor completely opaque tend to produce the **highest engagement and interest** (the "sweet spot" models of Berlyne and successors).
- Ambiguity sustains attention, invites re-interpretation, and keeps the emotional state open and evolving.
- Resolution of ambiguity can produce "insight pleasure" (aha moments), but **sustained ambiguity** can produce a distinct aesthetic emotion—sometimes called "aesthetic uncertainty" or "contemplative wonder."

This connects to the Stateful Viewers design choice of **preserving ambiguity** in reflections rather than resolving images into tidy interpretations.

### Key sources

- Berlyne, D. E. (1971). *Aesthetics and psychobiology*. Appleton-Century-Crofts.
- Jakesch, M., & Leder, H. (2009). Finding meaning in art: Preferred levels of ambiguity in art appreciation. *Quarterly Journal of Experimental Psychology*, 62(11), 2105–2112.
- Muth, C., & Carbon, C.-C. (2013). The aesthetic aha: On the pleasure of having insights into Gestalt. *Acta Psychologica*, 144(1), 25–30.

## 6) Emotion in art is not well served by basic emotion models

The "basic emotions" framework (Ekman's anger/fear/disgust/happiness/sadness/surprise) and dimensional models (valence + arousal) are useful starting points, but they **underspecify** aesthetic emotion:

- Many aesthetic emotions (being moved, awe, nostalgia, sublime, uncanny) are **blends** that don't reduce cleanly to single categories or simple dimensional coordinates.
- The **Geneva Emotional Music Scale** (GEMS) and related instruments for visual art identify emotion clusters specific to aesthetic contexts: wonder/transcendence, nostalgia/tenderness, peacefulness, power/energy, joyful activation, tension, sadness.
- Some researchers argue for **appraisal-based** models: what emotion arises depends on how the viewer evaluates the work along dimensions like novelty, coping potential, goal relevance, and norm compatibility.

### Implication for Stateful Viewers

The project's decision to use **qualitative, narrative state** rather than valence/arousal scores or emotion labels is well-supported by this literature. Narrative state can capture blends, ambiguity, and temporal unfolding in ways that categorical or dimensional labels cannot.

### Key sources

- Zentner, M., Grandjean, D., & Scherer, K. R. (2008). Emotions evoked by the sound of music: Characterization, classification, and measurement. *Emotion*, 8(4), 494–521.
- Scherer, K. R. (2005). What are emotions? And how can they be measured? *Social Science Information*, 44(4), 695–729.
- Silvia, P. J. (2009). Looking past pleasure: Anger, confusion, disgust, pride, surprise, and other unusual aesthetic emotions. *Psychology of Aesthetics, Creativity, and the Arts*, 3(1), 48–51.

## 7) Bodily and physiological dimensions of aesthetic emotion

Aesthetic emotions are not only mental—they have **embodied signatures**:

- **Chills (frisson)**: piloerection, shivers, often associated with being moved or peak aesthetic moments.
- **Tears and lump-in-throat**: associated with "being moved" in both sad and positive-valence contexts.
- **Skin conductance and heart rate changes**: markers of arousal that correlate with self-reported engagement.
- **Facial expressions**: subtle and often distinct from everyday expressions; "aesthetic smiles" and contemplative expressions have been documented.
- **Postural and respiratory changes**: less studied but consistent with the idea that aesthetic engagement involves the whole organism, not just cognition.

These bodily dimensions support the phenomenological claim (central to Stateful Viewers) that aesthetic experience is **embodied**, not just intellectual.

### Key sources

- Wassiliwizky, E., Wagner, V., Jacobsen, T., & Menninghaus, W. (2015). Art-elicited chills indicate states of being moved. *Psychology of Aesthetics, Creativity, and the Arts*, 9(4), 413–427.
- Merleau-Ponty, M. (2012). *Phenomenology of perception* (D. A. Landes, Trans.). Routledge. (Original work published 1945)

## 8) Individual differences: the same artwork does not evoke the same emotion in everyone

Large-scale studies consistently show **high variance** in emotional response to the same artwork:

- **Openness to experience** (Big Five) is one of the strongest personality predictors of aesthetic engagement and emotional intensity.
- **Art expertise** changes the balance of cognitive vs affective processing and can lead to more differentiated (but not necessarily more intense) emotional responses.
- **Mood state at entry** modulates what is attended to and how it is felt (congruence and incongruence effects).
- **Cultural background** shapes norms about what is "appropriate" to feel in response to art, and which aesthetic categories are salient.

### Relevance to Stateful Viewers

This is the theoretical basis for the **viewer profile** system. By generating diverse profiles with different baselines (emotional tone, ambiguity tolerance, attention style, aesthetic conditioning), the project explores how the same gallery sequence can produce divergent experiential trajectories.

### Key sources

- Chamorro-Premuzic, T., & Furnham, A. (2004). Art judgement: A measure related to both personality and intelligence? *Imagination, Cognition and Personality*, 24(1), 3–24.
- Vessel, E. A., Maurer, N., Denber, A. H., & Starr, G. G. (2018). Stronger shared taste for natural aesthetic domains than for artifacts of human culture. *Cognition*, 179, 121–131.

## 9) Emotion regulation and art: people use art to manage feeling

Beyond passive response, people actively use art for **emotion regulation**:

- Listening to sad music when sad (mood maintenance / savoring) is one of the most common self-reported uses.
- Museum visits, reading fiction, and looking at photographs can function as **affect regulation strategies**: calming, energizing, reframing, or simply allowing emotional states to be felt in a safe container.
- The concept of **"aesthetic distance"** (Bullough, 1912) suggests that art provides a frame within which emotions can be experienced without the urgency of real-world consequences.

This dimension is not currently modeled in Stateful Viewers, but it connects to possible extensions: a viewer whose regulatory goals (seeking consolation, seeking challenge, seeking distraction) shape how state evolves.

### Key sources

- Saarikallio, S., & Erkkilä, J. (2007). The role of music in adolescents' mood regulation. *Psychology of Music*, 35(1), 88–109.
- Bullough, E. (1912). "Psychical distance" as a factor in art and an aesthetic principle. *British Journal of Psychology*, 5(2), 87–118.

---

## 10) Neural correlates: what brain research adds (and doesn't)

Neuroimaging studies have identified several relevant patterns:

- **Default mode network (DMN) activation** during intense aesthetic experiences—suggesting self-referential and internally-directed processing, not just stimulus-driven response.
- **Reward circuitry** (ventral striatum, orbitofrontal cortex) is engaged by artworks rated as "beautiful" or "moving."
- **Amygdala and insula** activation during emotionally charged art—consistent with general emotion processing.
- **Prefrontal involvement** in evaluative judgments and meaning-making.

However, neural correlates **do not resolve** the richer questions: they confirm that art engages emotion circuits, but they don't tell us about the qualitative structure of the experience, the role of context, or temporal unfolding beyond the scale of a few seconds in a scanner.

### Key sources

- Vessel, E. A., Starr, G. G., & Rubin, N. (2012). The brain on art: Intense aesthetic experience activates the default mode network. *Frontiers in Human Neuroscience*, 6, 66.
- Chatterjee, A., & Vartanian, O. (2014). Neuroaesthetics. *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*, 18(7), 370–375.

---

## Summary: what this means for Stateful Viewers

The research literature supports several of the project's core commitments:

| Project commitment | Research support |
| --- | --- |
| Emotion is central to art perception | Strong empirical consensus |
| Emotional response unfolds temporally | Supported by temporal dynamics research and Dewey's process model |
| State carried forward from image to image | Consistent with priming, contrast, fatigue, and mood carry-over findings |
| Qualitative/narrative state over numeric scores | Supported by the inadequacy of basic-emotion and valence/arousal models for aesthetic contexts |
| Viewer profile shapes response | Supported by individual differences literature (openness, expertise, mood, culture) |
| Ambiguity is preserved, not resolved | Supported by optimal-ambiguity findings (Berlyne, Jakesch & Leder) |
| Embodied awareness as a dimension | Supported by bodily response research (chills, tears, physiological markers) |

The literature also points to dimensions **not yet modeled** that could enrich future work:

- **Emotion regulation goals** (why the viewer is looking)
- **Social context effects** (alone vs observed)
- **Aesthetic distance** as a dynamic, not just a given
- **Habituation and novelty curves** across a sequence
- **Mixed-emotion dynamics** (how blends evolve, not just how mood shifts)
